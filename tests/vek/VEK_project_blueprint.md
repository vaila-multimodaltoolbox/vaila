# vailá-ElasticKick (VEK)

## Product and Software Blueprint

**Project name:** vailá-ElasticKick  
**Acronym:** VEK  
**Proposed module:** `vek.py`  
**Platform:** [vailá Multimodal Toolbox](https://github.com/vaila-multimodaltoolbox/vaila)

---

## 1. Project Overview

The **vailá-ElasticKick (VEK)** is a biomechanical assessment module designed to quantify soccer or futsal kicking performance under elastic resistance.

The test combines:

1. markerless lower-limb kinematics;
2. elastic-band force estimation;
3. foot and ball velocity analysis;
4. mechanical power and work calculations;
5. trial-to-trial and athlete-to-athlete comparisons;
6. automated CSV, graph, and HTML report generation.

The system is intended to follow the general software architecture already used in:

- `vaila_and_jump.py`
- `vaila_deadlift.py`

The first implementation should be treated as an **experimental biomechanical assessment tool**, not yet as a diagnostic or normative instrument.

---

## 2. Scientific Rationale

Kicking performance is a complex task that depends on:

- proximal-to-distal coordination;
- hip motion;
- knee extension;
- ankle and foot velocity;
- timing of ball contact;
- ball velocity;
- strength and power;
- accuracy;
- technical execution.

The study:

> Vieira et al. (2016). *Kicking Performance and Muscular Strength Parameters with Dominant and Nondominant Lower Limbs in Brazilian Elite Professional Futsal Players*. Journal of Applied Biomechanics, 32(6), 578–585.

used variables such as:

- hip velocity;
- knee velocity;
- ankle velocity;
- foot velocity;
- ball velocity;
- accuracy;
- dominant versus nondominant limb comparisons.

The VEK extends this approach by adding a task-specific external resistance generated by an elastic band.

## 3. Main Objective

To develop a field-based and software-assisted test capable of estimating the mechanical and kinematic characteristics of a resisted soccer or futsal kick.

## 4. Specific Objectives

The VEK should:

- estimate elastic-band tension throughout the movement;
- calculate elastic-band deformation;
- calculate mechanical power against the band;

- compare dominant and nondominant limbs;
- compare multiple trials;
- generate individual and team reports;

- support GUI and CLI execution.

## 5. Important Terminology

The report should avoid using the generic term **kick force** unless force is directly measured by an instrument such as a force transducer.

Recommended terminology:

| Avoid | Recommended term |
|---|---|
| Kick force | Elastic-band tension |
| Kick power | Mechanical power against the elastic band |
| Ball force | Estimated ball impulse or ball kinetic energy |
| Maximum kick force | Maximum elastic-band tension |
| Contact force | Not reported unless directly measured |
| Ball power | Apparent mean power transferred to the ball |

## 6. Mechanical Model

### 6.1 Elastic-band deformation

Let:

- \(L(t)\) be the instantaneous elastic-band length;
- \(L_0\) be the resting or reference length;
- \(\Delta L(t)\) be the deformation.

\[
\Delta L(t) = L(t) - L_0
\]

---

### 6.2 Elastic-band force

A linear approximation may be used:

\[
F(t) = k \Delta L(t)
\]

where:

- \(F(t)\) is force in newtons;
- \(k\) is the elastic stiffness in N/m.

However, elastic bands are often nonlinear and may exhibit hysteresis. Therefore, the preferred model is:

\[
F(t) = f(L(t))
\]

where \(f\) is obtained from an experimental force-length calibration curve.

The software should support:

- linear calibration;
- polynomial calibration;
- monotonic interpolation;
- user-provided calibration tables.

---

### 6.3 Elastic-band extension velocity

\[
v_{\mathrm{band}}(t) = \frac{dL(t)}{dt}
\]

---

### 6.4 Instantaneous mechanical power against the elastic band

\[
P_{\mathrm{band}}(t) = F(t)\,v_{\mathrm{band}}(t)
\]

Positive power represents energy transferred by the athlete to stretch the band.

Negative power represents energy returned by the band or absorbed during deceleration.

---

### 6.5 Positive mechanical work

\[
W_{\mathrm{band}}^{+}
=
\int P_{\mathrm{band}}^{+}(t)\,dt
\]

where only positive power values are integrated.

---

### 6.6 Ball kinetic energy

\[
E_{\mathrm{ball}}
=
\frac{1}{2}m_{\mathrm{ball}}v_{\mathrm{ball}}^2
\]

where:

- \(m_{\mathrm{ball}}\) is ball mass;
- \(v_{\mathrm{ball}}\) is ball velocity immediately after impact.

---

### 6.7 Estimated ball impulse

For a stationary ball:

\[
J_{\mathrm{ball}}
\approx
m_{\mathrm{ball}}v_{\mathrm{ball}}
\]

This is an estimate of the ball linear impulse.

---

### 6.8 Apparent mean power transferred to the ball

\[
\bar{P}_{\mathrm{ball}}
=
\frac{E_{\mathrm{ball}}}{\Delta t_{\mathrm{contact}}}
\]

This should be labeled as **apparent mean power transferred to the ball**, because it is not equivalent to total muscular power.

---

### 6.9 Foot-to-ball speed ratio

\[
R_{v}
=
\frac{v_{\mathrm{ball}}}{v_{\mathrm{foot}}}
\]

This ratio may be used as an exploratory indicator of movement-to-ball transfer efficiency.

## 11. Recommended Signal Processing

### 11.1 Coordinate transformation

Markerless data may use screen coordinates, where the vertical coordinate increases downward.

For biomechanical plotting:

\[
y_{\mathrm{bio}} = 1-y_{\mathrm{screen}}
\]

when normalized coordinates are used.

---

### 11.2 Spatial scaling

Possible approaches:

- known shank length;
- known calibration object;
- homography;
- camera calibration;
- direct metric coordinates from another VAILA module.

---

### 11.3 Filtering

Suggested starting values:

| Signal | Suggested cutoff |
|---|---:|
| Human joint coordinates | 8–15 Hz |
| Foot trajectory | 10–18 Hz |
| Ball trajectory | 15–30 Hz |
| Band length | 8–15 Hz |

The optimal cutoff should be determined from:

- sampling rate;
- residual analysis;
- movement speed;
- motion blur;
- tracking noise.

The cutoff must remain below the Nyquist frequency.

A fourth-order zero-lag Butterworth low-pass filter is a reasonable initial implementation.

---

### 11.4 Numerical derivatives

Velocity:

\[
v(t) = \frac{dx(t)}{dt}
\]

Acceleration:

\[
a(t) = \frac{dv(t)}{dt}
\]

Recommended implementation:

- central differences;
- `numpy.gradient`;
- filtered position before differentiation;
- optional smoothing after differentiation.

---

## 12. Contact Event Detection

The contact event is critical.

The software should support multiple methods.

### Method 1: Manual frame

The user enters the impact frame.

This should be considered the reference method during initial validation.

---

### Method 2: Minimum foot-ball distance

\[
d(t)
=
\sqrt{
(x_{\mathrm{foot}}-x_{\mathrm{ball}})^2
+
(y_{\mathrm{foot}}-y_{\mathrm{ball}})^2
}
\]

The contact candidate is:

\[
t_{\mathrm{contact}}
=
\arg\min d(t)
\]

---

### Method 3: Ball acceleration onset

The contact frame may be estimated from the beginning of the rapid increase in ball velocity or acceleration.

---

### Method 4: Combined confidence score

A more robust implementation can combine:

- minimum foot-ball distance;
- increase in ball speed;
- foot deceleration;
- temporal proximity;
- tracking confidence.

Example:

\[
C_{\mathrm{contact}}
=
w_1C_d
+
w_2C_v
+
w_3C_a
+
w_4C_q
\]

where \(C_q\) represents tracking quality.

## 21. Normative Classification

The first version should not automatically label results as:

- excellent;
- good;
- average;
- poor.

Normative classifications require:

- a sufficiently large reference sample;
- sex-specific analysis;
- age-specific analysis;
- sport-level classification;
- reliability;
- validity;
- typical error;
- minimal detectable change;
- percentile definitions.

Until normative data are available, the software should use:

- raw values;
- within-athlete comparison;
- percentage difference;
- z-scores only when a valid reference dataset exists;
- trial ranking.

def lowpass_filter(
    values: np.ndarray,
    fps: float,
    cutoff_hz: float,
    order: int = 4,
) -> np.ndarray:
    values = np.asarray(values, dtype=float)

    if values.size < 8:
        return values.copy()

    nyquist = 0.5 * fps
    cutoff = min(cutoff_hz, nyquist * 0.95)

    if cutoff <= 0:
        raise ValueError("Cutoff frequency must be positive.")

    b, a = butter(order, cutoff / nyquist, btype="low")
    return filtfilt(b, a, values)


def calculate_speed(
    x: np.ndarray,
    y: np.ndarray,
    fps: float,
) -> np.ndarray:
    dt = 1.0 / fps
    vx = np.gradient(x, dt)
    vy = np.gradient(y, dt)
    return np.sqrt(vx**2 + vy**2)


def calculate_angle(
    proximal: np.ndarray,
    joint: np.ndarray,
    distal: np.ndarray,
) -> np.ndarray:
    vector_1 = proximal - joint
    vector_2 = distal - joint

    dot = np.sum(vector_1 * vector_2, axis=1)
    norm = np.linalg.norm(vector_1, axis=1) * np.linalg.norm(vector_2, axis=1)

    cosine = np.divide(
        dot,
        norm,
        out=np.full_like(dot, np.nan, dtype=float),
        where=norm > 0,
    )

    cosine = np.clip(cosine, -1.0, 1.0)
    return np.degrees(np.arccos(cosine))


def load_band_model(calibration_file: str | Path) -> PchipInterpolator:
    calibration = pd.read_csv(calibration_file)

    required = {"length_m", "force_n"}
    missing = required.difference(calibration.columns)

    if missing:
        raise ValueError(
            f"Band calibration file is missing columns: {sorted(missing)}"
        )

    calibration = calibration.sort_values("length_m")

    return PchipInterpolator(
        calibration["length_m"].to_numpy(dtype=float),
        calibration["force_n"].to_numpy(dtype=float),
        extrapolate=True,
    )


def calculate_band_length(
    ankle_x: np.ndarray,
    ankle_y: np.ndarray,
    anchor_x: float,
    anchor_y: float,
) -> np.ndarray:
    return np.sqrt(
        (ankle_x - anchor_x) ** 2
        + (ankle_y - anchor_y) ** 2
    )


def detect_contact_frame(
    foot_x: np.ndarray,
    foot_y: np.ndarray,
    ball_x: np.ndarray,
    ball_y: np.ndarray,
) -> int:
    distance = np.sqrt(
        (foot_x - ball_x) ** 2
        + (foot_y - ball_y) ** 2
    )
    return int(np.nanargmin(distance))

## 33. Scientific Limitations

The software should explicitly report that:

- markerless tracking may introduce coordinate error;
- 2D analysis is affected by out-of-plane motion;
- elastic bands may be nonlinear;
- hysteresis may affect force estimation;
- anchor movement invalidates the force model;
- ball contact duration is difficult to estimate from low-frame-rate video;
- ball velocity depends on tracking quality;
- power against the band is not total muscular power;
- force applied to the ball is not directly measured;
- normative interpretation requires an appropriate reference database.

---

## 34. Recommended Initial Report Wording

> The vailá-ElasticKick analysis estimates lower-limb kinematics, elastic-band tension, mechanical power against the band, mechanical work, foot velocity, and ball velocity from synchronized video-derived trajectories and an experimentally calibrated force-length relationship. The reported values are biomechanical estimates and should not be interpreted as direct measurements of muscular force or foot-ball impact force unless validated against dedicated force instrumentation.
